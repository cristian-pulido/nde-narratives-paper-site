Quantifying representational mismatch between narratives and structured reports in near-death experiences

Narratives preserve local expression and ambiguity, while questionnaires compress experience into structured retrospective summaries.

Cristian Pulido · Francisco Gómez · Prejaas K.B. Tewarie · Roxane S. Hoyer · Steven Laureys

📄Paper (Soon) 💻Code 🧪Demo

The Problem

Two valid representations of the same NDE can diverge: narrative text captures local expression, while questionnaires summarize the experience through predefined global categories.

Core Idea

  • Narratives preserve temporal flow, local tone, ambiguity, and distress.
  • Questionnaires formalize experience into standardized retrospective responses.
  • Both are informative, but they are not directly equivalent representations.
  • This work measures disagreement as a structured representational mismatch.

Abstract

  • Alignment is partial: experiential NDE-C is more recoverable than longer-term life changes in LCI-R.
  • Narrative tone is informative, but not a reliable proxy for global self-reported valence.
  • Strong benchmark performance supports a representational gap, not just a model limitation.

Example 1 · Local worry

"It was very bright... I was worried because I wanted to go downstairs and wake up, but I couldn't."

A globally positive report can still include locally distressing narrative segments.

Example 2 · Ambiguous language

"monochrome beige fixed image, in an attic, mushroom and wooden case. Don't calm down."

Fragmented expression weakens alignment between text tone and questionnaire labels.

Example 3 · Muted expression

"No pain, no voice, no reaction, total nothing."

Low-affect wording can appear neutral despite a positive retrospective appraisal.

1-Minute Summary

This study tests whether narrative-derived outputs and structured questionnaires align when describing near-death experiences. The core result is partial, structured alignment rather than full equivalence.

Question

  • Can narrative tone proxy self-reported valence?
  • Are experiential features easier to recover than long-term life changes?
  • Is disagreement model-driven or representational?

Main Findings

  • Alignment is systematic but incomplete across representations.
  • NDE-C recoverability is higher than LCI-R recoverability.
  • Tone is informative but not a reliable surrogate of lived valence.

Glossary

NDE-C: phenomenological content features reported during the experience.

LCI-R: longer-term life changes and after-effects reported after the event.

Macro F1: class-balanced metric that weights each class equally.

Cohen's kappa: agreement beyond chance between representations.

What Mismatch Is Not

  • It is not random noise.
  • It is not only model failure.
  • It does not invalidate narrative reports.

Two Representational Layers

  • Narrative layer: local episodes, sequence, ambiguity, mixed tone.
  • Questionnaire layer: global summary, predefined options, retrospective appraisal.

Family-Level Mismatch Pattern

Alignment is not uniform: NDE-C occupies the highest region, tone remains intermediate, and LCI-R after-effects cluster lower.

How to read: upper-right means stronger alignment between text-derived outputs and questionnaire labels.

Concrete Features Are More Recoverable

Experiential features are extracted more reliably than longer-term life changes, indicating different levels of linguistic recoverability.

How to read: circles trend higher than triangles, indicating stronger recoverability of phenomenological content.

Tone Is Informative, But Not Equivalent to Valence

Disagreement concentrates around mixed and neutral regions instead of direct positive-negative reversals.

How to read: disagreement clusters near ambiguous categories rather than polarity flips.

Benchmark Gap Supports the Main Claim

Higher agreement on standard sentiment datasets shows the NDE gap is mainly representational, not just model failure.

How to read: benchmark points occupy higher agreement regions than NDE tone results.

Human Reference Supports the Pattern

Human-reference validation reproduces the construct ordering: segmentation is most consistent, NDE-C remains more aligned than LCI-R, and tone stays intermediate.

How to read: similar ordering across human and automated comparisons indicates the mismatch is not explained by segmentation alone.

Preprocessing Acts as a Representational Filter

Translation and resegmentation improve structural clarity, but their effect is heterogeneous: NDE-C shifts the most, tone changes modestly, and LCI-R remains comparatively stable.

How to read: each dumbbell compares the complete pipeline against alternative preprocessing configurations.

Interpretation

What The Gap Suggests

  • Mismatch is systematic, not random.
  • Automated extraction operates on narrative expression, not on lived experience itself.
  • Disagreement reflects differences between reporting layers.

Why It Matters

  • The problem shifts from pure extraction toward elicitation and expression.
  • Improving how subjective experience is reported may matter as much as improving the models.
  • These limits likely extend beyond NDEs to other subjective domains.
